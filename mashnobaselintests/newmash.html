Great, now lets check our projection case: Suppose we generate a 44x44 centering matrix L and remove the final row:

R=ncol(b.train)
L=diag(R)-1/R*as.vector(rep(1,R))%*%t(as.vector(rep(1,R)))
L=L[-44,]

This matrix will transform \(b.train\) into a matrix of deviations from average expression across all R subgroups for R-1 tissues.

Recall that in our model,

\[Ceff_{R}=\mu_{R} + v_{R}\] \[Chat_{R} = \mu_{R} + v_{R} + E_{R}\]

where \[v_{R} \sim N(0,Uk)\] and \[E_{R} ~ N(0,V)\]

\[LCeff_{R-1} = (Lv)_{R-1}\] \[LChat = Lv + E*\]

where \[E_{R-1}* ~ N(0,LVL')\] and \[(Lv)_{R-1} ~ N(0,LUkL')\] so that \[(LChat)_{R-1}|V ~ N(0,LVL' + LUkL')\]

And as you can see, this transfomred matrix b.gp.hat represents the deviations from the mean across subgroups for n-1 subgroups (not the deviation from the mean of the n-1 subgroups, reassuringly), so the results are robust to which subgroup we omit.

all.equal(as.numeric(b.train[1,]-mean(as.numeric(b.train[1,])))[-20],as.numeric(b.gp.hat[1,]))
## [1] TRUE
plot(as.numeric(b.train[1,]-mean(as.numeric(b.train[1,])))[-20],as.numeric(b.gp.hat[1,]))

So essentially, for every j vector, we have transformed the observed averages to the deviation from observed (noisy averages) across all R subgroups, and then effectively omitted a subgroup. And reassuringly, it doesnt matter which subgroup we omit when we transform, because L still takes the deviation across all R subgroups (i.e., L is (R-1)xR).

And we can see that its deterministic, in that the 0-sum of the R-1 elements will represent the deviation of the omitted element:
